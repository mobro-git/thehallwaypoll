How decisive were these results?

Hat is the odd one out this week with three options, so it gets its own test; everything else is a straight two-way race.

TL;DR: Burn Care was the most one-sided call of the week — Aloe beat Moisturizing Lotion 57–15 (79%), with Sunscreen right behind it. Shades was the real toss-up: Aviator edged Wayfarer 38–34, about as close to a coin flip as it gets.

Baseball Cap isn’t just the leader, it’s the clear favorite: 63% of the vote. Wide-brimmed Floppy Hat (23%) pulls ahead of Bucket Hat (14%) for a distant second, no longer the even split it looked like before RTP weighed in. A chi-square goodness-of-fit test against “all three hats equally popular” gives p = 7.41^{-8} — nowhere close to what we’d expect if hat preference were random. Both offices have a type, too: HQ and RTP each independently put Baseball Cap on top.

Does HQ agree with RTP?

RTP’s votes are in for this poll, and it’s the most eventful HQ/RTP comparison we’ve had yet.

Burn Care Aloe 83% (35/42) 73% (22/30) 10% 0.4620
Method Reapply Sunscreen 70% (32/46) 52% (16/31) 18% 0.1760
Shades Aviator 48% (21/44) 61% (17/28) 13% 0.4040
Sunscreen Lotion 86% (43/50) 63% (19/30) 23% 0.0381

Two milestones this week. First, Sunscreen clears p < 0.05 for the first time since RTP joined the poll — HQ went 86% for Lotion, RTP only 63% (p = 0.038). Every office gap before this one had a p-value comfortably above 0.1 — this is the first one we can call a real difference rather than noise.

Second, the Alignment Index drops to 0.43 this week, its lowest reading yet (cumulative across the whole RTP era: 0.81, so the long-run picture is still solidly aligned — one rough week doesn’t undo five good ones). And Shades flipped outright: HQ’s real preference was Wayfarer, RTP’s was Aviator, and the combined total ended up siding with RTP. That brings the running tally to 8 questions since RTP joined where the two offices would have crowned different winners on their own.

Do the sun-safety-conscious cluster together?

Reapplying sunscreen and reaching for aloe after a burn both sound like the habits of the same sun-safety-minded person. Let’s bound the overlap.

Of the 72 overlapping voters, somewhere between 28 and 48 (39%–67%) could be both Reapply-Sunscreen and Aloe people, versus about 36 (49%) expected if the two habits were unrelated. Given how popular both options already are on their own, there’s a good floor under the “actually sun-safe” coalition — good news for everyone’s skin.
